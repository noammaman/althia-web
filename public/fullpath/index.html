How to read this

One thesis runs through both tasks.

The assignment is two problems — a hands-on copy debug and a strategic activation plan. I've treated them as one discipline: reason backward from a behavior to the rule that would prevent it, then build a system that measures that rule at scale.

Task 2 is where the mindset is concrete — four Lexi conversations, four precise fixes. Task 1 is where it scales — the same error-classification instinct becomes a diagnostic funnel, an evaluation stack, and a way to know whether a change actually worked. The connective tissue is a single evaluator taxonomy that grades both agents.

The through-line Most agent failures are a miscalibrated dial with two tails: doing too little (violating a user's stated limit) and doing too much (refusing safe, helpful ground). You'll see both tails in Lexi — and the whole point is that an evaluation system has to watch for both at once, because over-correcting one silently creates the other.

1Root cause

Lexi speaks British English to a Denver shopper: "boot space," "organise," "book you in," "sort it," "colour." An American car buyer reads this as off — at best quirky, at worst a signal they're texting a foreign call center or a bot, which erodes the local-dealership trust that is Lexi's entire job. The base model is defaulting to its training-distribution register because nothing in the prompt pins the locale. A softer secondary issue: the final message drifts into over-promising and clutter.

2Evaluator category
Localization / Dialect ConsistencyTone & Verbosity Drift ·secondary

Locale earns its own axis because it's cheap to detect — a lexicon/spelling check, no LLM needed — and high-signal for a US retail brand.

3Prompt remediation
Locale — US English only. You represent a dealership in the United States. Always use American English spelling and vocabulary; never British/Commonwealth terms. Say trunk not boot, color not colour, schedule/set up not organise, get you booked not book you in, take care of it not sort it. Prices in USD; US date format.

Pair with a deterministic banned-term lexicon in the eval pipeline so locale regressions are caught for free.

4Ideal response

Flip the toggle above to read it in context — American register, no over-promising, one forward-moving question to keep the visit alive.

+Interview defense note

Why locale as its own category, not just "tone"? Because the fix and the detector differ: tone drift needs an LLM judge and a rubric; locale is a closed lexical set you catch with a word-list at near-zero cost and 100% recall on known terms. Separating them keeps the eval stack cheap where it can be.

Edge case to prep for: a dealership near the Canadian border, or a bilingual market — locale rules should be configurable per dealer, not globally hardcoded. That foreshadows the "config over prompt-forking" theme in Task 1.

Constraint violation
I've got a Civic I'd want to put toward the new car. Looking for a 2011-2013 SUV, looking to spend $5,000, max $6,000.
We don't have SUVs from 2011-2013 in your $5,000-$6,000 range. Are you open to considering newer or different budget options?
Newer is ok but can't go over $7,000. I need all wheel drive.
actualI couldn't find any AWD SUVs priced under $7,000. Would you consider a new AWD pickup like the 2026 Chevrolet Colorado WT for $36,809?
idealThanks for the details — with AWD and a $7,000 cap, I'm not seeing a match in our used SUVs right now (most AWD SUVs on our lot start higher). Two ways I can help: I can flag you the moment something AWD comes in under $7k, and I can get your Civic appraised — a trade-in could stretch your budget and open up more options. Want me to start with the trade value?
Actual
Ideal
The ideal respects both hard constraints and revives the trade-in as the path forward.
1Root cause · three compounding failures

1. Hard-constraint violation (budget). The shopper set an explicit ceiling — max $7,000 — and Lexi recommended a vehicle 5.3× over budget. This is the cardinal sin: it says she isn't listening, and reads as a bait-and-switch. One message like this can end the conversation.

2. Body-style violation. They asked for an SUV; she offered a pickup. Two ignored constraints in one message.

3. Dropped context. The Civic trade-in — a material fact that changes their budget and is a conversion hook — is never acknowledged. Root cause: a missing constraint-handling and graceful-fallback policy.

2Evaluator category
Constraint Handling FailureContext RetentionRecommendation Relevance

"Recommended price ≤ stated max" is programmatically verifiable — a deterministic, high-severity check. Any violation is an automatic fail, no LLM judge required.

3Prompt remediation
Respect the shopper's stated constraints. Treat explicit limits — budget ceiling, body style, drivetrain, year — as hard requirements. Never recommend a vehicle that violates one. A price ceiling is absolute. When nothing matches: (1) say so plainly; (2) offer the closest in-constraint option, or one nearest out-of-constraint option only if you name the gap and ask permission; (3) never jump categories (SUV→pickup) without asking; (4) use known context — if they have a trade-in, factor it in.
4Ideal response

Toggle above: honest about the miss, respects both limits, and turns the trade-in into the next step instead of pushing an absurd upsell.

+Interview defense note

Why deterministic, not an LLM judge, for the budget check? "Price ≤ stated max" is a fact, not a judgment — cheaper, faster, 100% precision, and it can gate deploys in CI. Reserve the judge for the fuzzier question of whether she degraded gracefully.

Trade-off considered: you could argue an over-budget car is legitimate "anchor high" selling. Over SMS with an explicit stated cap, the trust cost outweighs it — and the rule already allows a stretch option with permission.

Edge case: soft vs. hard constraints — "around $6k" is negotiable, "max $6,000" is not. The policy has to read the modifier, which is exactly the nuance I'd put in the Task 1 golden set.

1Root cause · two linked failures

Verbosity / sycophancy drift. The first reply is pure filler and, over SMS where replies should be a sentence or two, reads unmistakably as a bot — while burying the fact that she never answered.

Silent dead-end / false async promise. "Let me check… please hold on" implies a synchronous lookup that never resolves — there's no human pausing, and she never calls the inventory tool. This is the costlier failure: verbosity annoys, but the dead-end loses the lead. Root cause: no SMS brevity constraint, plus a missing "act, don't narrate" rule.

2Evaluator category
Tone & Verbosity DriftDead-End DeflectionTool-Use Failure

Both are tagged co-primary. Verbosity is what a human notices first; the dead-end is what actually kills the conversion — and they need different detectors.

3Prompt remediation
Keep it short (SMS). Reply in 1–2 sentences, under ~30 words. Answer the shopper's actual question first. No filler praise, no stacked adjectives, one question back max. Act, don't narrate. If a tool can look something up, call it and give the result — don't say "let me check" and stop. If you truly can't get the answer in this channel, say so plainly and give the next step. Never promise a check you won't complete in the same turn.
4Ideal response

Toggle above: checks inventory, gives the real answer, moves to booking — in a fraction of the words.

+Interview defense note

Why split one bad message into two categories? Different fixes, different detectors: verbosity is catchable with a cheap word/adjective heuristic; the dead-end needs a judge checking "did the agent answer or advance the task?" Tagging both lets prioritization see the dead-end is the conversion-killer even though verbosity is more visible.

Trade-off: a hard word cap risks clipping legitimately complex answers (financing math, multi-vehicle comparisons) — so it's a strong default, not an absolute; the golden set should include cases where a longer reply is correct.

Edge case: real tool latency — a genuine "checking now" message is fine only when a call is actually in flight, never as a substitute for it.

1Root cause

Over-refusal / guardrail over-application. There's a legitimate guardrail — Lexi shouldn't quote rates or approve financing — but she applies it to the entire topic rather than the specific commitments. A high-intent, time-bound lead ("this month") gets a cold brush-off on the exact concern blocking the sale.

Compounded by no empathy or bridging: she never acknowledges the concern, never reassures at the safe general level, never gives a reason to still come in. Note the deliberate mirror to Conv 2 — that was under-respecting a constraint; this is over-respecting one. You can't fix one by cranking the other.

2Evaluator category
Over-Refusal / Guardrail Over-ApplicationEmpathy & Rapport MissLost Conversion

Tracked as a distinct axis from constraint-violation precisely because they're opposite tails of the same calibration dial.

3Prompt remediation
Financing — reassure and route, don't quote or refuse the topic. You may not quote specific rates, APRs, payments, or approval decisions, and must not promise terms. But financing is a welcome topic — never shut it down. When a shopper raises it: (1) acknowledge warmly; (2) reassure generally — the dealership works with a wide range of budgets, down payments, and credit situations; (3) collect any context they offer; (4) route to a specialist as a positive next step, framed as getting real numbers. Never reply only "I can't help with that."
4Ideal response

Toggle above: validates the concern, reassures inside safe bounds, keeps the Passport alive, converts the hand-off into momentum.

+Interview defense note

Isn't this encouraging the agent to give financial advice? No — the rule tightens what's forbidden (specific numbers, approvals, promises) while permitting the general reassurance any good salesperson gives. The failure wasn't too little caution; it was caution aimed at the wrong target.

Why pair it with Conv 2? To show the taxonomy is calibrated in both directions — over- and under-application are separate categories with separate fixes, and a system that watches only one silently creates the other.

Edge case: jurisdiction — some financing statements carry regulatory weight, so the "safe reassurance" language should itself be reviewed by compliance and pinned, not improvised by the model.

+Why tie a dealership agent to a SaaS builder?

Different products, same job. Both tasks are the same core competency — evaluating an LLM agent's behavior by reasoning backward from what it did to the rule that fixes it. Task 2 is that discipline on a single conversation; Task 1 is the same discipline built into a system that watches ten thousand.

Methodology crosses; domain logic doesn't. The builder's failures stay builder-native — ignored a "weekdays only" condition, S3 tool-calling errors, activation failures, trust-drop to the legacy UI. Only the classification discipline transfers. The automotive and SaaS domains stay separate, exactly as they should.

And the transfer is partial, on purpose. Localization ("boot" vs "trunk") and SMS verbosity barely exist in the builder; constraint-handling, clarification, dead-ends, and over-refusal clearly do. Mapping only what genuinely transfers — and saying which parts don't — is the point. A forced 1:1 mapping would be the tell of shoehorning; a partial one is the tell of actually understanding both.

Task 1 · AI Workflow Builder

From 30% to 55% activation in a quarter.

The builder lets small-business users describe an automation in plain language; today 30% end up saving and activating something that works. Getting to 55% is four moves: diagnose, assess at scale, prioritize, validate. First, the assumptions I'd confirm on day one — so every decision is traceable to one.

Stated assumptions

  • Volume~8,000 builder sessions / quarter. Enough to A/B large effects, not to slice thin segments — this shapes the whole measurement approach.
  • ArchitectureA graph of nodes: intake/NLU → clarification → plan/spec → tool-calling (trigger, actions, field mapping) → validation/preview → save & activate. I can read graph traces, not just chat text.
  • Outcome label"Saved & activated a working automation" is a clean binary, plus a "fell back to the UI" signal. This is ground truth.
  • "Working"Passes validation and runs/enables — not merely "saved."
  • BandwidthSome eng time for instrumentation, an eval harness, and feature-flagged A/B. No full graph re-architecture this quarter.
1.1

Diagnosing what's not landing.

Two questions, two methods. Where users drop is quantitative (telemetry). Why they drop is qualitative (log clustering). Most teams do only the first and optimize blind.

Where · instrument the graph as a funnel

Turn the graph into a measurable funnel and emit an event at each stage. Drop-off per stage tells you where the 70% leaks — and a leak at S1 (never understood them) needs a completely different fix than one at S3 (tool failed) or S5→S6 (built it but didn't trust it enough to activate).

Two stages carry most of the loss: the agent-side tool-calling leak at S3 (a prompt/architecture fix) and the user-side trust drop at S5→S6 — built it, didn't activate it (a trust/UX fix). Same funnel, two very different remedies — which is the whole point of labeling by actor.

Two refinements that change what you learn 1 · Capture the exit type, not just the exit stage. Why · cluster the failing sessions

Telemetry says S3 is bleeding; it can't say why. For the leaking stages: embed → cluster → label → human-verify. Group similar failures automatically, name the clusters, and hand-read a stratified sample so you trust them rather than taking the machine's word. That produces a failure taxonomy for the builder — the same category set from Task 2, reused.

Reused from Lexi

Intent misunderstanding · clarification failure (over/under) · capability & grounding gap · constraint violation · silent dead-end — each maps straight onto a Lexi conversation.

Builder-specific

Tool-calling errors (malformed args, field mapping, auth) · validation/activation failure · trust drop / fallback-to-UI.

+Interview defense note

Why a funnel before reading logs? Reading logs without a funnel is fishing — you find vivid anecdotes and mistake them for the main leak. The funnel tells you where to spend expensive human/LLM attention.

Why both clustering and a fixed taxonomy? Clustering catches modes you didn't anticipate (open-world); the taxonomy makes results comparable over time (closed-world). Running only one blinds you in one direction — the same over/under lesson from Task 2.

Edge case: attribution — an S3 auth failure may be the partner's outage, not the agent's fault. The taxonomy must separate "agent caused" from "environment caused" or you'll spend a quarter tuning prompts against a broken integration.

If pressed on clustering mechanics: embeddings → UMAP → HDBSCAN is the standard stack; PCA is a fast linear alternative for the reduction step, but UMAP preserves the local structure semantic clusters live in. That's an ML-team implementation detail — my job is defining what a "failure cluster" means and validating it's real.

1.2

Assessing usage at scale.

You can't hand-read thousands of sessions, and you can't fly blind on metrics. So the framework is a tiered eval stack — cheap-and-broad at the bottom, expensive-and-deep at the top — where each layer catches what the one below can't.

A distinction worth saying out loud · discovery comes first Discovery precedes measurement. Vectorization + clustering is discovery — unsupervised, "what failure modes even exist?" — and it's what builds the taxonomy. LLM-as-judge is measurement — it applies that taxonomy to score each session. A category has to be discovered and named before the judge can grade against it, so the order is always cluster → name → judge. What makes them feel simultaneous is the flywheel in steady state: you measure known categories with the judge while the human loop keeps discovering new, unnamed modes that feed back as new categories. Both run at once — but for any single category, discover-then-measure. (And the judge reads raw transcript text, not the clustering vectors.)

+Interview defense note

Why not one great LLM judge? Cost, latency, trust. Anything programmable (locale lexicon, price ≤ max, turn count) should never touch an LLM. The judge is reserved for genuine judgment.

Why the gold-set / κ obsession? The judge is itself a model that can drift or be wrong. Tune the agent against an unvalidated judge and you optimize toward its blind spots — you need a human anchor.

Trade-off: this is more infra than "read some logs," so I'd sequence it — Layer A + a small gold set in week one, judge and clustering as they earn their keep — not all four layers before the first fix ships.

Quick wins · high impact, low effort

Prompt + clarification-policy fixes: intent-capture phrasing, "act don't narrate," constraint-respect, over-refusal calibration. Cheap, fast, reversible — and exactly the Task 2 lessons applied to the builder. Do these first.

Impact

Share of sessions hitting the failure × estimated lift × stage weight. Earlier leaks compound — a 10-pt fix at S1 can beat a 20-pt fix at S5, because everyone downstream had to survive S1 first. This weighting is the non-obvious move.

Confidence

How sure the fix works and how cleanly it's measurable. A prompt tweak validated on the golden set + A/B is high-confidence; "improve trust" is low until it has a metric.

Effort

The real tell: prompt (hours) < graph tweak (days) < new tool/integration (weeks). Most activation problems are reachable in the prompt before touching architecture.

My actual sequencing logic Attack the biggest early-funnel leak that's prompt-addressable first — highest impact, lowest effort, fastest to measure. Ship behind a flag, read the result, reinvest. Resist opening with a glamorous architectural fix: prompt iterations teach you faster and cheaper, and each one sharpens the eval stack for the harder bets.
+Interview defense note

Why stage-weight impact instead of raw frequency? A funnel is multiplicative — fixing an early stage feeds every later one, so the same point-lift is worth more the earlier it sits.

Isn't prompt-first just easy-mode? It's expected-value: prompt fixes are cheap, reversible, and fast to A/B, so they have the best information-per-day return — and they de-risk expensive bets by revealing the true ceiling first.

Edge case: some leaks genuinely aren't prompt-fixable (missing integration, broken auth). The matrix has to be honest about those so you don't burn three prompt iterations on a problem the prompt can't reach — the builder-side echo of Conv 2's "closest real option" honesty.

1.4

Knowing whether it actually worked.

The north-star is unambiguous — activation 30% → 55% on comparable cohorts — but a single top-line number is how teams fool themselves. Validation runs offline before shipping, online after, with guardrails built to catch fake wins.

Offline · before shipping

Golden set — must improve the target category without regressing others (the CI gate). Kills most bad changes in minutes.

Replay / counterfactual — re-run the new prompt against real historical failed sessions. If it wouldn't have saved the sessions it targets, it doesn't ship.

Held-out, not trained-on — score on a slice of sessions I didn't inspect while writing the fix. Grading a change on the exact sessions used to design it is training on the test set: it overfits and flatters the result. Split into a development set (inspectable) and a held-out evaluation set (scoring only) — the gate number always comes from held-out.

Synthetic benchmark — generated conversations grounded in the real log distribution (incl. a user-simulator for multi-turn) to stress-test coverage. Supplements replay; never replaces it, since simulated users are cleaner than real ones.

Online · the real verdict

Feature-flagged A/B, randomized by account — the only thing that proves causation on the live metric.

Primary: activation rate, control vs. treatment. Guardrails (fake-win detectors): turn count & time-to-activate, fallback-to-UI rate, and downstream retention — is the automation still active two weeks later?

Rigor: pre-register the hypothesis, compute sample size / MDE first (8k/quarter detects large effects, not 1-pt moves), run to significance, watch novelty effects.

Guarding against Goodhart The moment "activation" is the target, there's pressure to game it — nudging users to save a half-broken automation. That's why "working & still-active after N days" is baked into the definition and guardrails. Measure the outcome you want, not the proxy that's easy to inflate.
Attribution honesty 55% may not be reachable by prompt changes alone — some of the gap could be missing integrations or the legacy-UI pull. Tracking must separate "agent got better" from "we shipped a connector," so leadership reads the right lever.
Validation isn't an event — it's the flywheel's steady state Post-ship, the eval stack keeps running as a regression monitor: funnel, judge tags, and guardrails on a live dashboard, so a later change that quietly re-breaks constraint-handling gets caught by the same system that measured the win.
+Interview defense note

Why offline replay before A/B? Live traffic is scarce and slow (8k/quarter); you can't A/B every idea. Offline replay is the cheap filter that earns a change its spot in the limited A/B pipeline.

Why obsess over retention? Activation is a proxy, and every proxy is gameable. The two-week-active check re-anchors on the true goal and defuses Goodhart.

Edge case: interference and segments — account-level randomization avoids within-team contamination; I'd pre-commit to a couple of segment cuts (new vs. returning, simple vs. complex) while resisting slicing until something looks significant by chance.

Debug the one conversation. Build the system that measures it across ten thousand.
